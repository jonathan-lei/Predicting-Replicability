<div class="sectionInfo"><h2 class="sectionHeading">METHOD</h2></div><p><span class="sentence">Twenty-five non-Black undergraduates participated for course credit or a payment of $10. </span><span class="sentence">They sat unrestrained about 30 in. from an ASL 504 remote eye-tracking camera (Applied Science Laboratories, Bedford, MA) at the base of a 17-in. </span><span class="sentence">LCD display. </span><span class="sentence">A Dell desktop computer calculated point of gaze and passed the information every 33 ms to a PowerMac G4, which controlled stimulus presentation and collected gaze-duration data. </span><span class="sentence">Following a calibration routine (2–5 min), participants read: “Please watch the following discussion. </span><span class="sentence">At the conclusion of the discussion you will be asked questions about the discussion content and/or the discussion participants.” </span><span class="sentence">The prerecorded video then began (<span class="figure refFigure figuresContent" id="ref-figure1-j1467-9280200802072x">Fig. 1a</span>), and participants' eye movements were tracked for its duration.</span></p><div class="figure" id="figure1-j.1467-9280.2008.02072.x"><div class="holder"><img alt="&#10;                        figure&#10;                    " src="/paper_data/img/10.1111_j.1467-9280.2008.02072.x-fig1.gif"/><div class="caption"><p><span class="captionLabel">Fig. 1.</span> The video screen as it appeared to participants (a) and mean looking time to each discussant during the potentially offensive comments (b). Results are shown separately for the headphones-on and headphones-off conditions. Error bars indicate standard errors. The <i>p</i> value indicates a significant effect of the headphones manipulation (Tukey honestly significant difference).</p></div></div></div><p><span class="sentence">At the start of the video, an off-screen voice established that either all discussants could hear each other (<i>headphones on</i>) or the bottom two discussants (which included the Black individual) could not hear the others (<i>headphones off</i> ). </span><span class="sentence">To reinforce this information, discussants raised their hands if they could hear each other, and one affirmed, “So, it's just two of us now?” or “So, it's all four of us now.” </span><span class="sentence">The footage was otherwise identical in the two conditions.</span></p><p><span class="sentence">In the video, the discussants were instructed to give their thoughts on the university's admissions policies. </span><span class="sentence">The first White discussant responded that the university should consider having admission interviews. </span><span class="sentence">The second White discussant criticized affirmative action in a way that was potentially offensive (<span class="figure refFigure figuresContent" id="ref-figure1-j1467-9280200802072x">Fig. 1a</span>). </span><span class="sentence">Whereas the association hypothesis predicted that the Black individual would be looked at more than the available White discussants in both conditions, the social-referencing hypothesis predicted this response only in the headphones-on condition, when he could hear the remark and have a potentially informative reaction.</span></p>
