<div class="sectionInfo"><h2 class="sectionHeading">STUDY 2: GODS</h2></div><p><span class="sentence">Philosophers can vaguely note that religion is an opiate for the masses (Marx, 1844/1959), but psychologists need to explain more specifically which pain people might take this drug to alleviate. </span><span class="sentence">Belief in religious agents can serve to alleviate several psychological problems (e.g., awareness of one's own mortality; Norenzayan &amp; Hansen, 2006), and we predicted that one of those is the pain of social disconnection (see also Burris, Batson, Altstaedten, &amp; Stephens, 1994; Kirkpatrick et al., 1999). </span><span class="sentence">People who are induced to feel disconnected from other humans should therefore report a stronger belief in religious agents than those induced to feel socially connected.</span></p><p><span class="sentence">Not all religious agents, of course, are conceptualized as the kind one might willingly seek when in need of social connection. </span><span class="sentence">The overwhelming majority of religious systems contain both loving supernatural agents (e.g., God) who perform desirable actions (e.g., miracles) and evil supernatural agents (e.g., Devil) who perform unpleasant actions (e.g., curses). </span><span class="sentence">Social disconnection could increase belief in some religious agents or actions but not in others. </span><span class="sentence">Such a result seems unlikely, however, given the high intercorrelation between belief in one religious agent within a tradition and belief in other agents within that same tradition. </span><span class="sentence">Belief in God within a Judeo-Christian tradition, for instance, entails belief in the Devil as well. </span><span class="sentence">We measured belief in a variety of religious agents and associated actions in Study 2, however, to examine this issue.</span></p><div class="head-b"><h3>Method</h3></div><p><span class="sentence">A pool of potential participants from the University of Chicago completed an on-line survey that included the question, “Do you believe in God?” </span><span class="sentence">Ninety-nine of these people (57 female, 42 male)—50 believers who responded “yes” and 49 nonbelievers who responded “no”—were then invited to participate in Study 2 in exchange for $7.</span></p><p><span class="sentence">After arriving at the laboratory, participants first completed a computerized version of the 90-item Eysenck Personality Questionnaire (Eysenck &amp; Eysenck, 1976) and were then told that the computer would generate a set of future-life predictions based on their responses. </span><span class="sentence">To bolster participants' belief in the accuracy of the predictions, we told them that the personality questionnaire was a measure of extraversion and provided accurate feedback on the 12-item Extraversion subscale.</span></p><p><span class="sentence">We then manipulated social connection using a procedure developed by Twenge, Baumeister, Tice, and Stucke (2001). </span><span class="sentence">All participants received a paragraph of “future life predictions” ostensibly based on their personality profile. </span><span class="sentence">Those in the disconnected condition read statements suggesting that they would be lonely in their lives (e.g., “You're the type who will end up alone later in life”). </span><span class="sentence">Those in the connected condition read statements suggesting that they would be socially connected in their lives (e.g., “You're the type who has rewarding relationships throughout life”). </span><span class="sentence">Approximately half of the participants within each experimental condition were religious believers, and the other half were nonbelievers.</span></p><p><span class="sentence">Participants were then asked to “begin Part 2 of the study,” in which they rated the extent to which they believed in ghosts, angels, the Devil, miracles, curses, and God, on scales ranging from 1 ( <i>not at all</i>) to 10 ( <i>very much</i>).</span></p>
