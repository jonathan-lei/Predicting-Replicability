<span class="sentence">      In five studies, the authors examined the effects on cognitive performance of coherence and incoherence between conceptual and experiential sources of affective information. </span><span class="sentence">The studies crossed the priming of happy and sad concepts with affective experiences. </span><span class="sentence">In different experiments, these included approach or avoidance actions, happy or sad feelings, and happy or sad expressive behaviors. </span><span class="sentence">In all studies, coherence between affective concepts and affective experiences led to better recall of a story than did affective incoherence. </span><span class="sentence">The authors suggest that the experience of such experiential affective cues serves as evidence of the appropriateness of affective concepts that come to mind. </span><span class="sentence">The results suggest that affective coherence has epistemic benefits and that incoherence is costly in terms of cognitive performance.</span>
