<span class="sentence">      Researchers interested in counterfactual thinking have often found that upward counterfactual thoughts lead to increased motivation to improve in the future, although at the cost of increased negative affect. </span><span class="sentence">The present studies suggest that because upward counterfactual thoughts indicate reasons for a poor performance, they can also serve as excuses. </span><span class="sentence">In this case, upward counterfactual thoughts should result in more positive self-esteem and reduced future motivation. </span><span class="sentence">Five studies demonstrated these effects in the context of self-handicapping. </span><span class="sentence">First, upward counterfactual thinking was increased in the presence of a self-handicap. </span><span class="sentence">Second, upward counterfactual thoughts indicating the presence of a self-handicap protected self-esteem following failure. </span><span class="sentence">Finally, upward counterfactual thoughts that protect self-esteem reduced preparation for a subsequent performance as well as performance itself. </span><span class="sentence">These findings suggest that the consequences of upward counterfactuals for affect and motivation are moderated by the goals of the individual as well as the content of the thoughts.</span>
