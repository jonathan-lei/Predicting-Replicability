<div class="sectionInfo"><h2 class="sectionHeading">EXPERIMENTS 1 AND 2</h2></div><p><span class="sentence">Experiments 1 and 2 transposed one of the visual experiments reported by Phillips (1974) to the auditory domain. </span><span class="sentence">On each trial, the listener was presented with two successive “chords” (sums of pure tones) and had to indicate if they were identical or not. </span><span class="sentence">When the two chords were not identical, the difference resulted from a frequency shift in a single, randomly selected tone, as in the example given in <span class="figure refFigure figuresContent" id="ref-figure1-j1467-9280200802050x">Figure 1</span> (left). </span><span class="sentence">Performance, measured using the detectability index <i>d</i>′, was examined as a function of two variables: the number of tones in each chord (<i>N</i>) and the ISI.</span></p><div class="figure" id="figure1-j.1467-9280.2008.02050.x"><div class="holder"><img alt="&#10;                        figure&#10;                    " src="/paper_data/img/10.1111_j.1467-9280.2008.02050.x-fig1.gif"/><div class="caption"><p><span class="captionLabel">Fig. 1.</span> Illustration of the task used in Experiments 1 and 2 (left) and in Experiments 4 and 5 (right). Dashes represent pure tones. The correct responses on these two trials are “different” (left) and “downward” (right).</p></div></div></div><div class="head-b"><h3>Method</h3></div><div class="head-c"><h4>Participants</h4></div><p><span class="sentence">Four audiometrically normal listeners, including authors L.D. and W.T., participated in both experiments. </span><span class="sentence">Each listener was initially trained in the task for at least 4 hr. </span><span class="sentence">This training period was used to adjust the magnitude of the frequency shifts to listener-specific values, so as to prevent floor or ceiling effects.</span></p><div class="head-c"><h4>Stimuli</h4></div><p><span class="sentence">The pure tones forming each chord had a duration of 600 ms and a nominal sound pressure level (SPL) of 65 dB; they were gated on and off with 20-ms raised-cosine amplitude ramps. </span><span class="sentence">In the first chord of each trial, adjacent tones were separated by frequency intervals varying randomly (and uniformly, on a logarithmic frequency scale) between 3 and 6 semitones (1 semitone = 1/12 octave). </span><span class="sentence">The first chord was randomly positioned in a 5-octave frequency range (125–4000 Hz) and was followed by the second chord after a silent ISI of 0, 250, 750, or 2,000 ms. From the first chord to the second chord, the probability of a frequency shift in one tone was .5; when such a shift occurred, it was equiprobably positive or negative. </span><span class="sentence">The tones forming the two chords had random initial phases, but any tone appearing in both chords had the same initial phase in these two chords.</span></p><p><span class="sentence">In Experiment 1, the value of <i>N</i> was 4, 7, or 12. </span><span class="sentence">The probability of a shift was equal for all tones, and the magnitude of the shifts, Δ<i>s</i>, was fixed for a given listener. </span><span class="sentence">As <span class="figure refFigure figuresContent" id="ref-figure2-j1467-9280200802050x">Figure 2a</span> indicates, Δ<i>s</i> was equal to 0.50 semitone for 3 listeners and 0.70 semitone for the 4th listener.</span></p><div class="figure" id="figure2-j.1467-9280.2008.02050.x"><div class="holder"><img alt="&#10;                        figure&#10;                    " src="/paper_data/img/10.1111_j.1467-9280.2008.02050.x-fig2.gif"/><div class="caption"><p><span class="captionLabel">Fig. 2.</span> Results of Experiments 1, 2, 4, and 5: mean values of <i>d</i>′ as a function of the interstimulus interval (ISI) and the number of tones in the chords (<i>N</i>). For each experiment, a table displays the magnitude of the frequency shifts, Δ<i>s</i> (in semitones), as a function of <i>N</i> for each tested listener (L1, L2, … L7; in Experiment 4, Δ<i>s</i> did not vary across listeners).</p></div></div></div><p><span class="sentence">In Experiment 2, the value of <i>N</i> was either 4 or 12, and only “inner” tones could shift (so that only 2 tones could shift when <i>N</i> was 4, whereas 10 tones could shift when <i>N</i> was 12). </span><span class="sentence">In this experiment, moreover, we used larger Δ<i>s</i> values for 12-tone chords than for 4-tone chords, as indicated in <span class="figure refFigure figuresContent" id="ref-figure2-j1467-9280200802050x">Figure 2b</span>, in order to obtain similar overall levels of performance for these two conditions.</span></p><p><span class="sentence">The stimuli were digitally generated with a sampling rate of 44.1 kHz and a 24-bit amplitude quantization. </span><span class="sentence">They were presented binaurally via headphones (Sennheiser HD265).</span></p><div class="head-c"><h4>Procedure</h4></div><p><span class="sentence">Trials were arranged in blocks of 40, with <i>N</i> and the ISI fixed within each block. </span><span class="sentence">The listener, seated in a sound-attenuating booth, gave his or her responses (“same” or “different”) by clicking a computer mouse on two virtual buttons. </span><span class="sentence">When the ISI was 0 or 250 ms, the delay separating a response from the first chord of the next trial was 600 ms; otherwise, this delay was equal to the ISI. </span><span class="sentence">Every session included one block of trials in each of the 12 (Experiment 1) or 8 (Experiment 2) possible conditions, which were randomly ordered. </span><span class="sentence">Each experiment consisted of 12 sessions.</span></p>
