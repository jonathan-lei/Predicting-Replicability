<div class="sectionInfo"><h2 class="sectionHeading">EXPERIMENT 1: COLOR AND OBJECT NAMING IN FRENCH</h2></div><div class="head-b"><h3>Method</h3></div><div class="head-c"><h4>Participants</h4></div><p><span class="sentence">Thirty-two native speakers of French participated in this experiment. </span><span class="sentence">Half of them performed the color-naming task, and half the object-naming task. </span><span class="sentence">All were students at the Université de Provence, France. </span><span class="sentence">They received course credit for participation.</span></p><div class="head-c"><h4>Materials and Design</h4></div><p><span class="sentence">Four colors (red, green, blue, and orange) were used. </span><span class="sentence">For each color, we selected seven pictures (Alario &amp; Ferrand, 1999) that shared at least the onset consonant with the color name. </span><span class="sentence">These color-object pairings were used for the congruent condition. </span><span class="sentence">The incongruent condition was created by re-pairing all pictures having a given onset with a particular color whose name had a different onset (e.g., all “r” pictures were re-paired with the same nonred color), so that picture names and color names did not overlap in phonology (see Table 1 for a list of the object names). </span><span class="sentence">Thus, there were 56 experimental items. </span><span class="sentence">An additional set of 56 filler items was created by pairing a set of 28 pictures with two different color names that did not overlap with the object names in phonology. </span><span class="sentence">Thus, overall, there were 28 congruent and 84 incongruent trials. </span><span class="sentence">Finally, eight of the filler items used in the experiment proper were also used during training. </span><span class="sentence">The original pictures were black line drawings on a white background; they were transformed into colored pictures by coloring the outline with one of the four designated colors.</span></p><div class="NLM_table-wrap"><div class="caption"><p><span class="captionLabel">TABLE 1</span> <i>Names of the Stimulus Objects</i></p></div><img src="/paper_data/img/10.1111_j.1467-9280.2008.02070.x-table1.jpeg" alt="" id="_i5"/></div><p><span class="sentence">Items were pseudorandomly ordered within four blocks of trials. </span><span class="sentence">Each block consisted of an equal number of experimental and filler trials, with each picture appearing once, and the different colors appearing an equal number of times. </span><span class="sentence">The same color never appeared on consecutive trials, and neither did pictures with a semantic or phonological relationship. </span><span class="sentence">There were never more than three items from the same condition in a row. </span><span class="sentence">The order of the blocks was counterbalanced following a Latin-square design.</span></p><div class="head-c"><h4>Procedure</h4></div><p><span class="sentence">Participants sat in front of a computer with an attached microphone and performed either the object- or the color-naming task. </span><span class="sentence">The experimental software was DMDX (Forster &amp; Forster, 2003). </span><span class="sentence">For both tasks, participants first were familiarized with the objects and their names. </span><span class="sentence">On each trial, they saw a sequence consisting of a fixation cross (700 ms), a blank screen (200 ms), a picture (1,000 ms), and finally the picture with its name (1,000 ms). </span><span class="sentence">The appearance of the picture name cued participants to name the picture aloud. </span><span class="sentence">Participants in the color-naming task were then told the four color names that would be used in the subsequent two phases. </span><span class="sentence">After familiarization, participants were trained and then performed the experiment proper. </span><span class="sentence">These two phases had identical trial structures: a fixation cross (700 ms), followed by a blank screen (200 ms), followed by a picture of a colored object (1,500 ms). </span><span class="sentence">Depending on the instructions, participants named the object or the color (with the adjective unmarked for grammatical gender) upon picture presentation. </span><span class="sentence">There were eight trials in the training phase. </span><span class="sentence">The experiment lasted 25 min.</span></p>
