<span class="sentence">      A model of risk regulation is proposed to explain how low and high self-esteem people balance the tension between self-protection and connectedness goals in romantic relationships. </span><span class="sentence">This model assumes that interpersonal risk automatically activates connectedness and self-protection goals. </span><span class="sentence">The activation of these competing goals then triggers an executive control system that resolves this goal conflict. </span><span class="sentence">One correlational study and 8 experiments manipulating risk, goal strength, and executive strength and then measuring implicit and explicit goal activation and execution strongly supported the model. </span><span class="sentence">For people high in self-esteem, risk triggers a control system that directs them toward the situations of dependence within their relationship that can fulfill connectedness goals. </span><span class="sentence">For people low in self-esteem, however, the activation of connectedness goals triggers a control system that prioritizes self-protection goals and directs them away from situations where they need to trust or depend on their partner.</span>
