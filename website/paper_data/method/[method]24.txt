<h3>Experiment 1</h3><br/><p class="body-paragraph" data-auto="body_paragraph"><button class="sentence">Experiment 1 compares recall performance of lists of phonologically similar items with recall of lists of dissimilar items. </button><button class="sentence">The major difference between this and other published studies is the number of participants.</button></p> <h4>Method</h4><p class="body-paragraph" data-auto="body_paragraph"><strong>Participants</strong></p><p class="body-paragraph" data-auto="body_paragraph"><button class="sentence">A total of 100 Purdue University undergraduates who were native speakers of American English participated in exchange for course credit.</button></p><p class="body-paragraph" data-auto="body_paragraph"><strong>Stimuli</strong></p><p class="body-paragraph" data-auto="body_paragraph"><button class="sentence">The to-be-remembered items were 64 one-syllable words used by Surprenant, Neath, and LeCompte (1999) to study PSEs. </button><button class="sentence">An example similar list is <em>vote, boat, goat, float, note</em>, and <em>coat</em>. </button><button class="sentence">An example dissimilar list is <em>break, sick, vote, greet, rat</em>, and <em>fun</em>. </button><button class="sentence">Each word appeared in both similar and dissimilar lists (e.g., <em>vote</em> in the above example). </button><button class="sentence">Lists were randomized such that participants saw the same words but in different random orders.</button></p><p class="body-paragraph" data-auto="body_paragraph"><strong>Procedure</strong></p><p class="body-paragraph" data-auto="body_paragraph"><button class="sentence">Participants were informed that we were interested in how accurately they could remember the order in which a series of words had been presented. </button><button class="sentence">Each word was shown in uppercase, center justified, for 1.5 s in the middle of the screen in 20-point Helvetica font. </button><button class="sentence">After the final word was shown, six response buttons became active and were labeled with the six words in alphabetical order. </button><button class="sentence">The participants were asked to indicate the presentation order by clicking on appropriately labeled buttons using the mouse. </button><button class="sentence">For example, if they thought the first word was <em>break</em>, they should click on the button labeled <em>break</em> first. </button><button class="sentence">If they thought the third word was <em>vote</em>, they should click on the button labeled <em>vote</em> third. </button><button class="sentence">Participants received 20 lists, half with dissimilar and half with similar items, and were informed they could take rest breaks at any point. </button><button class="sentence">The order of dissimilar and similar trials was randomly determined for each participant. </button><button class="sentence">Participants were tested individually, and an experimenter remained in the room to ensure compliance with the instructions.</button></p>
