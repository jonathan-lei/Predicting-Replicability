<div class="sectionInfo"><h2 class="sectionHeading">STUDY 1</h2></div><p><span class="sentence">In this study, participants were primed with spatial closeness or spatial distance using a Cartesian-plane coordinate system. </span><span class="sentence">They then read an embarrassing book excerpt and rated the extent to which they liked the excerpt. </span><span class="sentence">We hypothesized that participants primed with distance would like the embarrassing story more, and those primed with closeness would like the story less, compared with people primed with an intermediate amount of distance.</span></p><div class="head-b"><h3>Method</h3></div><div class="head-c"><h4>Participants</h4></div><p><span class="sentence">Seventy-three undergraduates (41 female, 32 male) were randomly assigned to the three spatial-prime conditions.</span></p><div class="head-c"><h4>Materials and Procedure</h4></div><p><span class="sentence">As a cover story, participants were told that the experimenters were interested in obtaining feedback on materials for a new type of standardized test. </span><span class="sentence">To produce spatial-distance cues, we asked participants to mark off two points on a Cartesian plane (see <span class="figure refFigure figuresContent" id="ref-figure1-j1467-9280200802084x">Fig. 1</span>). </span><span class="sentence">Each participant was given one of three sets of coordinates, which served as the closeness, intermediate, or distance prime. </span><span class="sentence">The set of coordinates for the closeness prime, (2, 4) and (−3, −1), were close to each other on the coordinate plane. </span><span class="sentence">The coordinates for the distance prime, (12, 10) and (−11, −8), were far from each other on the coordinate plane. </span><span class="sentence">The coordinates for the intermediate prime, (8, 3) and (−6, −5), were roughly halfway between those used for the closeness and distance primes. </span><span class="sentence">In other words, within the frame of the Cartesian plane, the points used in the closeness manipulation were relatively close to each other, and the points used in the distance manipulation were relatively far apart.</span></p><div class="figure" id="figure1-j.1467-9280.2008.02084.x"><div class="holder"><img alt="&#10;                        figure&#10;                    " src="/paper_data/img/10.1111_j.1467-9280.2008.02084.x-fig1.gif"/><div class="caption"><p><span class="captionLabel">Fig. 1.</span> The grid used for spatial priming. Participants were told, “Please locate the following points on this grid.” The coordinates were (2, 4) and (−3, −1) in the closeness condition, (8, 3) and (−6, −5) in the intermediate condition, and (12, 10) and (−11, −8) in the distance condition.</p></div></div></div><p><span class="sentence">Afterward, participants read an excerpt, taken from the book <i>Good in Bed</i> (Weiner, 2001), that depicted embarrassment. </span><span class="sentence">In this excerpt, the protagonist discovers a magazine article written about her. </span><span class="sentence">The article, titled “Loving a Larger Woman,” was written by the protagonist's ex-boyfriend.</span></p><p><span class="sentence">Participants answered three questions that assessed the extent to which they liked the excerpt. </span><span class="sentence">Each response was made using a scale ranging from 1 ( <i>not at all</i>) to 9 ( <i>extremely</i>). </span><span class="sentence">Specifically, participants were asked: “How much did you <i>enjoy</i> the passage?” “How <i>interested</i> might you be in reading more of the story that the passage is from?” and “Did you find the passage <i>entertaining</i>?” </span><span class="sentence">Responses to these three items were averaged into a reliable index of liking (α = .88).</span></p><p><span class="sentence">Finally, participants were probed for suspicion using the funneled debriefing technique (Bargh &amp; Chartrand, 2000). </span><span class="sentence">Three participants indicated suspicion that the Cartesian-plane task primed notions of distance (though they showed no awareness of the study's hypothesis); their data were removed from the analyses.</span></p>
